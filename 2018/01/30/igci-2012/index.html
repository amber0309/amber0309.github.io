Theoretical Generalization of Information Geometric Causal Inference

Posted on 2018-01-30 |

This post summarizes the logic of the journal version of Information Geometric Causal Inference [1] .

Independence postulate

The idea of Information Geometric Causal Inference (IGCI) is built upon the independence between $P_{Y|X}$ and $P_{X}$, where $X$ and $Y$ denote the cause and effect, respectively without loss of generality. IGCI interprets this independence in the following postulate

Postulate 1 (General structure of independence) Assume that $X$ causes $Y$ ($X \to Y$). Let $x \mapsto h(x) \in \mathbb{R}$ be any function for which $h(x)$ describes local properties of the conditional $P_{Y|X}$ at the point $X = x$. Then the structure function $h$ and $P_{X}$ are likely to satisfy
\begin{align}
\int h(x) P(x) dx \approx \int h(x) U_{X}(x) dx, \tag{1}
\label{post:1}
\end{align}

where $U_{X}(x)$ is a reference density of $X$.

Note that the difference between both sides of \eqref{post:1} can be rephrased as a covariance if we formally consider $h$ and $P_{X}/U_{X}$ as functions of a random variable $X$ with distribution $U_{X}$:
\begin{align}
&\int h(x) P(x) dx - \int h(x) U_{X}(x) dx \\
= \; & \int h(x) \frac{P(x)}{U_{X}(x)} U_{X}(x) dx - \int h(x) U_{X}(x) dx \int \frac{P(x)}{U_{X}(x)} U_{X}(x) dx \\
=: \; & \text{Cov}_{U_{X}} \left( h, \frac{P(x)}{U_{X}(x)} \right). \tag{2}
\label{cov}
\end{align}

The postulate is still vague since the structure function $h$ and the reference density are not specified. In the paper, the authors recommend to choose non-informative distributions to be the reference such as uniform or Gaussian ones. They define the candidate structure functions as follows:
Definition 1 (Structure functions for the conditional) Let $U_{X}$ and $U_{Y}$ be reference densities for $X$ and $Y$, respectively and
\begin{align}
\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} := \int P(y|x) U(x) dx
\end{align}

denote the output distribution obtained by feeding the conditional with the reference input $U_{X}$. Similarly, we will later use
\begin{align}
\overleftarrow{P}_{X} := \int P(x|y) U(y) dy.
\end{align}

Then we define the following structure functions
\begin{align}
h_{1}(x) & := \int P(y|x) \log \frac{ P(y|x) }{ U(y) } dy = D(P_{Y|x} \Vert U_{Y}), \\
h_{2}(x) & := \int P(y|x) \log \frac{ P(y|x) }{ \overrightarrow{P}_{Y} } dy = D(P_{Y|x} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}), \\
h_{3}(x) & := \int P(y|x) \log \frac{ \overrightarrow{P}_{Y} }{ U(y) } dy \\
&= \int P(y|x) \log \frac{ P(y|x) }{ U(y) } dy - \int P(y|x) \log \frac{ P(y|x) }{ \overrightarrow{P}_{Y} } dy \\
& = h_{1}(x) - h_{2}(x) ,
\end{align}

where $D(P \Vert Q)$ is the relative entropy (or KL-divergence) [2] between two densities $P$ and $Q$.

Orthogonality in information space

To derive the relation between those densities in the information space, we need the following Lemma
Lemma 1 (orthogonality in information space) Let $q,r,s$ be three probability densities. Then
\begin{align} \int q(x) \log \frac{r(x)}{s(x)} dx= \int r(x)\log \frac{r(x)}{s(x)}dx, \tag{3}
\label{ortho}
\end{align}

which is equivalent to the following additivity of KL divergence
\begin{align} D(q \Vert s) = D(q \Vert r) + D(r \Vert s). \end{align}

The above condition is called orthogonality between $\vec{qr}$ and $\vec{rs}$ [3]. Intuitively, it embodies that the vector formed by densities $q$ and $r$ is orthogonal to the one formed by $r$ and $s$ in the space of probability densities.

Using Lemma 1, the independence postulate of different structure functions in Definition 1 can be formulated as shown in the following theorem.

Theorem 1 (Orthogonality in information space) The conditions $\text{Cov}_{U_{X}}(h_{i}, P_{X}/U_{X})$ for $i = 1, 2, 3$ are equivalent to
\begin{align}
h_{1}: \; & D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} U_{Y})
= D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} P_{Y \vert X}) + D(U_{X} P_{Y \vert X} \Vert U_{X} U_{Y}), \\
h_{2}: \; & D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} \overrightarrow{P}_{Y})
= D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} P_{Y \vert X}) + D(U_{X} P_{Y \vert X} \Vert U_{X} \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}), \\
h_{3}: \; & D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) = D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}) + D(\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}). \tag{4}
\label{info-ortho}
\end{align}

Proof. For $h_{1}$, there is
\begin{align}
& \text{Cov}_{U_{X}}(h_{1}, P_{X} / U_{X}) \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int h_{1}(x) P_{X} dx - \int h_{1}(x) U_{X} dx = 0 \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int \left[ \int P_{Y \vert X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{U_{Y}} dy \right] P_{X} dx = \int \left[ \int P_{Y \vert X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{U_{Y}} dy \right] U_{X} dx \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{U_{Y}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{U_{Y}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X} P_{Y, X} U_{X}}{U_{Y} P_{Y, X} U_{X}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X} U_{X}}{U_{Y} U_{X}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y, X} }{U_{Y} U_{X}} dxdy - \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y, X} }{P_{Y \vert X} U_{X}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X} U_{X}}{U_{Y} U_{X}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} U_{Y}) = D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} P_{Y \vert X}) + D(U_{X} P_{Y \vert X} \Vert U_{X} U_{Y}).
\end{align}

For $h_{2}$,
\begin{align}
& \text{Cov}_{U_{X}}(h_{2}, P_{X} / U_{X}) \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int h_{2}(x) P_{X} dx - \int h_{2}(x) U_{X} dx = 0 \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int \left[ \int P_{Y \vert X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}} dy \right] P_{X} dx = \int \left[ \int P_{Y \vert X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}} dy \right] U_{X} dx \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X} P_{Y, X} U_{X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} P_{Y, X} U_{X}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X} U_{X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} U_{X}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y, X} }{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} U_{X}} dxdy - \int P_{Y, X} \log \frac{P_{Y, X} }{P_{Y \vert X} U_{X}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{P_{Y \vert X} U_{X}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} U_{X}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} \overrightarrow{P}_{Y})= D(P_{Y,X} \Vert U_{X} P_{Y \vert X}) + D(U_{X} P_{Y \vert X} \Vert U_{X} \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}).
\end{align}

For $h_{3}$,
\begin{align}
& \text{Cov}_{U_{X}}(h_{3}, P_{X} / U_{X}) \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int h_{3}(x) P_{X} dx - \int h_{3}(x) U_{X} dx = 0 \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int \left[ \int P_{Y \vert X} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dy \right] P_{X} dx = \int \left[ \int P_{Y \vert X} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dy \right] U_{X} dx \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y \vert X} P_{X} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dxdy = \int P_{Y \vert X} U_{X} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dxdy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dy = \int \overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} P_{Y}}{U_{Y} P_{Y}} dy = \int \overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & \int P_{Y} \log \frac{P_{Y} }{U_{Y}} dy - \int P_{Y} \log \frac{P_{Y}}{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}} dy = \int \overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}} dy \\
\Leftrightarrow \; & D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) = D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}) + D(\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}).
\end{align}

Q.E.D.

Identifiability of bijection relations

From this section on, we elaborate the identifiability results when the relation between $X$ and $Y$ is bijective, i.e. $Y = f(X)$ where $f$ is invertible. Note that orthogonality $h_{3}$ is the only one that is applicable to the deterministic case since it only refers to the image of the uniform input density under the conditional $P_{Y \vert X}$. Equivalent formulations of condition \eqref{info-ortho} are shown in the following theorem:

Theorem 2 (Equivalent formulations of orthogonality $h_{3}$) For bijective relations, the following conditions are equivalent:
(i) Orthogonality $h_{3}$ in Theorem 1:
\begin{align}
D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) = D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}) + D(\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}).
\end{align}

(ii) Uncorrelatedness between input and transformed density:
\begin{align}
\text{Cov}_{U_{X}}( \log \frac{\overleftarrow{P}_{X}}{U_{X}}, \frac{P_{X}}{U_{X}}) = 0.
\end{align}

(iii) Transformed orthogonality:
\begin{align}
D(P_{X} \Vert \overleftarrow{P}_{X}) = D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}) + D(U_{Y} \Vert \overleftarrow{P}_{X}).
\end{align}

(iv) Additivity of irregularities:
\begin{align}
D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) = D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}) + D(\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}). \tag{5}
\label{irgl}
\end{align}

(v) Additivity of approximation error:
\begin{align}
D(P_{X} \Vert \overleftarrow{P}_{X}) = D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}) + D(\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}).
\end{align}

Proof. \eqref{irgl} is the one that actually used in practice so I only show its proof here. Since KL-divergence is conserved under bijections, $D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}) = D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y})$. \eqref{irgl} is then obtained by replacing $D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y})$ with $D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X})$ in \eqref{info-ortho}. Q.E.D.

Using the orthogonality, one can obtain the following violations in the anti-causal direction.

Theorem 3 (Violations in backward direction) Let $f$ be non-trivial in the sense that the image of $U_{X}$ under $f$ does not coincide with $U_{Y}$. If one condition (and thus all) in Theorem 2 holds, then the corresponding conditions that exchange the role of X and Y are violated with definite sign:
\begin{align}
& D(P_{X} \Vert \overleftarrow{P}_{X}) + D(\overleftarrow{P}_{X} \Vert U_{X}) > D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}), \\
& \text{Cov}_{U_{Y}}( \log \frac{\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}}{U_{Y}}, \frac{P_{Y}}{U_{Y}}) > 0, \\
& D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) + D(U_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}) > D(P_{Y} \Vert \overrightarrow{P}_{Y}), \\
& D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) + D(\overleftarrow{P}_{X} \Vert U_{X}) > D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}), \label{asym} \tag{6} \\
& D(P_{X} \Vert \overleftarrow{P}_{X}) + D(\overleftarrow{P}_{X} \Vert U_{X}) > D(P_{Y} \Vert\overrightarrow{P}_{Y}).
\end{align}

Proof. \eqref{asym} corresponds to \eqref{irgl} in Theorem 2. By reordering \eqref{irgl} we have
\begin{align}
D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}) & = D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) - D(\overrightarrow{P}_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) \\
& < D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}) + D(\overleftarrow{P}_{X} \Vert U_{X})
\end{align}

Q.E.D.

Causal inference method

Observing that \eqref{irgl} implies
\begin{align}
D(P_{X} \Vert U_{X}) \le D(P_{Y} \Vert U_{Y}),
\end{align}

whenever $X \to Y$ in bijective relations. Therefore the inference method reads:

Information Geometric Causal Inference. Let $\mathcal{E}_{X}$ and $\mathcal{E}_{Y}$ be manifolds of smooth reference distributions for $X$ and $Y$, respectively. Consider the distances of $P_{X}$ and $P_{Y}$ to $\mathcal{E}_{X}$ and $\mathcal{E}_{Y}$, respectively, as the complexity of the distributions. Define the complexity loss from $P_{X}$ to $P_{Y}$ by
\begin{align}
C_{X \to Y} := D(P_{X} \Vert \mathcal{E}_{X}) - D(P_{Y} \Vert \mathcal{E}_{Y}).
\end{align}

Likewise, the loss from $P_{Y}$ to $P_{X}$ is given by exchanging the roles of $X$ and $Y$. Then, infer that $X \to Y$ if $C_{X \to Y} < 0$, or that $Y \to X$ if $C_{X \to Y} > 0$.

The following lemma shows how $C_{X \to Y}$ can be can be estimated in practice.

Lemma 2 ($C_{X \to Y}$ as difference of Shannon entropies) Let $P_{X}$ and $P_{Y}$ be densities on $\mathbb{R}^{d}$. Assume that $U_{X}$ and $U_{Y}$ are the projections
of $P_{X}$ on $\mathcal{E}_{X}$ and $P_{Y}$ on $\mathcal{E}_{Y}$, respectively. Then
\begin{align}
C_{X \to Y} & = (S(U_{X}) - S(U_{Y})) - (S(P_{X}) - S(P_{Y})) \\
& = (S(U_{X}) - S(P_{X})) - (S(U_{Y}) - S(P_{Y}))
\end{align}

Proof. Since $U_{X}$ is the projection of $P_{X}$ onto $\mathcal{E}_{X}$, we have
\begin{align}
D(P_{X} \vert \mathcal{E}_{X}) & = D(P_{X} \vert U_{X}) \\
& = \int P_{X} \log \frac{P_{X}}{U_{X}} dx \\
& = \int P_{X} \log P_{X} dx - \int P_{X} \log U_{X} dx \\
& = -S(P_{X}) - \int P_{X} \log \frac{U_{X}}{U^{(0)}_{X}} dx \\
& = -S(P_{X}) - \int U_{X} \log \frac{U_{X}}{U^{(0)}_{X}} dx \\
& = -S(P_{X}) + S(U_{X}),
\end{align}

where $U^{(0)}_{X}$ is the uniform distribution and the second last equality uses Lemma 1. Similarly, $D(P_{Y} \vert \mathcal{E}_{Y}) = -S(P_{Y}) + S(U_{Y}). Q.E.D.
